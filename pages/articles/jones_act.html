The Jones Act: Balancing U.S. Maritime Security and Economic Costs

What is the Jones Act?

In an attempt to rebuild the U.S. shipping industry post-World War 1, Washington Senator Wesley Jones introduced the Merchant Marine Act, which was signed into law by President Woodrow Wilson on June 5, 1920. The Merchant Marine Act, also known as the Jones Act, aimed to reduce reliance on foreign-built ships in an attempt to create jobs and business revenue for the U.S. To do this, it places restrictions on foreign boat shipments to non-continental U.S. territories like Hawaiʻi, Alaska, and Puerto Rico. Since the establishment of the Jones Act, there have been debates over whether the economic benefits of the law outweigh the detrimental effects.

What are the benefits?

American Maritime Partnership (AMP) is a U.S. maritime industry lobbying coalition and a proponent of the Jones Act, asserting that it is “critical to our country’s economic security.” One benefit it cites is that the Jones Act ensures a pool of trained domestic mariners and a fleet of vessels are ready to assist the Pentagon in times of crisis. The law also increases security by requiring that vessels operating between U.S. ports remain under U.S. regulatory oversight. Additionally, AMP highlights economic impacts, stating that these policies have supported the construction of more than 40,000 vessels in American shipyards and sustained approximately 650,000 jobs across all 50 states, contributing $154 billion annually to the U.S. economy.

What are the problems?

On the opposing side of this debate, many residents of non-continental U.S. regions have voiced complaints about the increased cost of living associated with strict shipping requirements. According to the Cato Institute, the Jones Act limits foreign competition, allowing domestic shipping companies to charge higher prices for transporting goods to places like Hawaiʻi, Alaska, and Puerto Rico. Because these regions have smaller populations and fewer shipping routes, there are fewer ships and less frequent service, which makes transporting goods more expensive. The requirement that vessels operating between U.S. ports be U.S.-built and U.S.-crewed further increases costs, which are ultimately passed on to consumers. This combination of factors contributes to higher prices for groceries, fuel, construction materials, and other essentials compared with the continental United States. The Cato Institute also points to inefficiencies in oil transportation, noting that in some cases it may be cheaper to import oil from regions like Russia or the Middle East than to ship it domestically (for example, sending it to Texas before distribution). Based on data from AAA Fuel Prices, Hawaiʻi experiences some of the highest gas prices in the country, despite being a very rural state. However, supporters of the law dispute the extent of these impacts, arguing that geographic isolation, infrastructure limitations, and global market forces also play a role in shaping prices in these regions.

The "Island Tax"

Many Hawaiʻi residents have faced rising living costs partly attributed to the Jones Act and are currently seeking a way to lessen this issue. The Cato Institute has proposed a few solutions that could help to mitigate the so-called “island tax” and improve purchasing power for local families. Their most direct recommendation is to abolish the Jones Act completely; but, given the political and logistical challenge of doing so, they also propose a practical alternative that could make places like Hawaiʻi, Alaska, and Puerto Rico more affordable to live in, while keeping the law in placeIf removing the Jones Act proves unsuccessful, they suggest loosening certain restrictions specified by the law; for example, allowing American companies purchase cheaper and modern ships from allies such as Japan and South Korea, as their shipbuilding costs are around 4 to 5 times cheaper than U.S. ships.
